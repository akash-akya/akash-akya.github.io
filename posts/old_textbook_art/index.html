Art in Old Textbook

06 May 2021

I recently got into digging old Kannada books from archive.org. By old I mean long before my great grandfather was born. Some of them are dated 1865! While the language, topic, even the examples they take sounds raw, odd, or weird. But it’s not difficult read and understand. On the contrary, I think more people might understand this language than the current one we use in publishing. Part of it might be because of the lesser usage of Sanskrit words which is more in the current publishing. And since at that time there was no “standard language for publishing” each book uses its own dialect and tone. You can even map the region of the dialect since it’s very similar to current spoken regional dialects.

Main reason I got interested in old books apart of language is that, a book is essentially a window to different time-space. We get to see a world from a wildly different eyes. Sometimes their worldview can be odd, offensive from today’s viewpoint. And other times it appears so familiar. It is interesting to think how the generation after 150 years will look at us! Anyway, I’ll save the rant for some other time. For now, let’s see these photo-realistic arts I found in a first standard textbook published in 1906.

The composition and details suggest that these are based on real photographs. My initial guess was that these were halftone prints. But it is not, these are created from different stroke patterns of varying size, producing a much more detailed print than halftone can produce. My best guess is its created using Stipple engraving technique. A skilled artist might have created the engravings by tracing the original photograph. What makes it even more interesting is that all these effort they put into a first standard textbook in 1906 for a regional language in India. I don’t think many kids even went to school at that time, let alone having books. The quality of the print is no less than modern books. From the scan, we can clearly see that the prints are very clean without any sort of smudge which was common in my childhood text books which was published 100 years after this. I couldn’t find any artifacts in the print.
